Some space to think:
Students reflect on their study habits in a post-pandemic world
By Nira Dayanim

Our definition of workspace has morphed and broadened since the Covid-19 pandemic first sent us home over two years ago. When offices and academic institutions first shuttered their doors and moved online in March 2020, those on both ends of the Zoom had to adjust their habits to meet the new normal’s demands. For some students, these changes to study habits and patterns have lasted longer than expected.

Prior to the pandemic, David Charendoff, a 22-year-old mechanical engineering student at the University of Maryland, did the majority of his work in Mckeldin Library’s study carrels. Charendoff struggles to focus and liked how the environment pushed him to concentrate.

When he got back to school last fall, Charendoff was excited to jump into his routine, but found it more difficult than anticipated. Now a senior, Charendoff still hasn’t returned to his former favorite location. A combination of Mckeldin only being available in a limited capacity last year and Zoom burnout kept him studying in his room. “I haven’t been in Mckeldin for probably my entire senior year,” he said. “I just fell out of the habit.”



Online school and social isolation led to increased burnout and anxiety in college students across America, according to a study conducted at Ohio State university. Reported anxiety and burnout increased by over 30% between August 2020 and April 2021, the study found.

Shira Laserson, a 22-year-old community health major, experienced mental health struggles due to the pandemic firsthand. A naturally extraverted person, Laserson’s work ethic was impacted as she struggled with burnout when faced with more alone time than she was used to.

One benefit Laserson found was that the pandemic changed many of her peers and teachers’ outlook on mental health, changing the academic environment. ”I don’t know if it’s because of the pandemic or just a shift in society but people are a lot more open about admitting when they’re struggling.”

According to Laserson, while being affected by world events drained her, because they relate to the content of her major, they also motivated her academically. “I’ve seen how communities hurt together, but I realized during everything how important it was to learn ways to help them heal together too,” said Laserson.
