Students reflect on their study habits in a post-pandemic world

Some space to think:

Students reflect on their study habits in a post-pandemic world

By Nira Dayanim

Our definition of workspace has morphed and broadened since the Covid-19 pandemic first sent us home over two years ago. When offices and academic institutions first shuttered their doors and moved online in March 2020, those on both ends of the Zoom had to adjust their habits to meet the new normal’s demands. For some students, these changes to study habits and patterns have lasted longer than expected.

Prior to the pandemic, David Charendoff, a 22-year-old mechanical engineering student at the University of Maryland, did the majority of his work in McKeldin Library’s study carrels. Charendoff struggles to focus and liked how the environment pushed him to concentrate.

When he got back to school last fall, Charendoff was excited to jump into his routine, but found it more difficult than anticipated. Now a senior, Charendoff still hasn’t returned to his former favorite location.

A combination of McKeldin only being available in a limited capacity last year and Zoom burnout kept him studying in his room. “I haven’t been in McKeldin for probably my entire senior year,” he said. “I just fell out of the habit.”



Shira Laserson, a 22-year-old community health major, also experienced burnout due to the pandemic . A naturally extraverted person, Laserson loves to do work around other people. Her work ethic was impacted and she struggled with anxiety when faced with more alone time than she was used to.

Online school and social isolation led to increased burnout and anxiety, affecting students across America, according to a study conducted at Ohio State university. According to surveys done by Mental Health America, the percentage of adults with any mental illness has been on the rise in Maryland for the last six years, peaking during the pandemic.




Shira Laserson, a 22-year-old community health major, experienced mental health struggles due to the pandemic firsthand. A naturally extraverted person, Shira's work ethic was impacted as she struggled with burnout when faced with more alone time than she was used to. Now that things have adjusted back to in person, Shira's habits are returning to normal. She spends a lot of time studying in her apartment building’s workspace, she said.

For Wendy Kellman, a 23-year-old psychology major, pandemic-induced changes in her study patterns have proved permanent. Wendy primarily used to study with other people in lecture halls and libraries across campus. When things went online, that stopped abruptly.

Wendy's classes have mainly stayed online and her bedroom has become her primary study space. “I'm just not as out and about as I used to be. I spend most of my time in the apartment,” said Wendy. “I haven’t socially studied since the pandemic started. Even in my apartment I don’t do homework with friends anymore,” Wendy said.

Though adjusting to the pandemic was difficult for David and Shira, now that they’re nearing the other side, they’ve noticed positive changes in the academic environment.

Shira found that the pandemic changed many of her peers and teachers’ outlook on mental health, changing the academic environment. ”I don’t know if it’s because of the pandemic or just a shift in society but people are a lot more open about admitting when they’re struggling.”

David, an observant Jew, found that the pandemic has also changed people’s perspectives on religious accomodations. “Everyone’s already learned how to use Zoom, so like, if I ask a teacher to record a lecture for me when there’s a holiday or something, they’re much more understanding about it.”

David also found that this flexibility has allowed him to return home to his family in New York for the holidays. “There were times when I’d stay in school even when I really wanted to be home, and now because people have learned to be flexible, I have this freedom.”

According to Shira, while being affected by world events drained her, as a community health major, they also motivated her academically. “I’ve seen how communities hurt together, but I realized during everything how important it was to learn ways to help them heal together too,” Shira said.
